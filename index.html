James Webb's birthday is coming up

The launch of the James Webb will be one year old next Christmas. A small time, given the revolution this telescope has already brought to humanity. It is also perhaps NASA's most ambitious project to date, as it is the largest telescope in size and weight currently.

Comparing the more famous Hubble telescope with the James Webb, the big difference between them would be that its younger brother sees in the infrared spectrum, making it possible to capture galaxies and stars 13.5 billion light-years away.

Why James Webb's so revolutionary

Every space telescope observes the "past", since the light emitted by distant stars and planets needs a certain amount of time to reach the Earth. For example, when we observe the light from a star located 1,000 light years away, we are observing the light that was emitted by the star 1,000 years ago. The James Webb can also contribute to surveys of the planets outside our Solar System. The telescope's infrared is sensitive to molecules such as water, carbon dioxide, and organic substances, which gives it the power to see the molecular composition of atmospheres on planets and moons in the Solar System and on exoplanets.

According to NASA, the telescope consists of four large scientific instruments. The first is the Fine Guidance Sensor/Near Infrared Detector and Slit-less Spectrograph. It enables the James Webb to have high quality imaging accuracy and is used to investigate first light detection, characterization, and transit of exoplanets. The Mid-Infrared Instrument has a camera and spectrograph that sees light in the mid-infrared region, with wavelengths longer than our eyes can see. With its sensitive detectors, it will be possible to see red-shifted light from distant galaxies, newly formed stars, and faint comets. The Near Infrared Spectrograph is used to scatter light from an object into a spectrum. In this way it is possible to discover physical properties such as temperature, mass, and chemical composition of an object. This instrument is designed to observe 100 objects simultaneously and is the first spectrograph in space with this capability. Finally, the Near Infrared Camera is the telescope's main imaging camera. It will detect light from early stars and galaxies in the process of formation, as well as stars in nearby galaxies. The NIRCam has coronagraphs, i.e. instruments that block the light from a brighter object, making it possible to photograph darker objects nearby.

Top 3

James Webb's most impressive achievements in 2022. (Images in the section below).

1 - “Cosmic Cliffs” in the Carina Nebula

Called the Cosmic Cliffs, the region is actually the edge of a gigantic, gaseous cavity within NGC 3324, roughly 7,600 light-years away. The cavernous area has been carved from the nebula by the intense ultraviolet radiation and stellar winds from extremely massive, hot, young stars located in the center of the bubble, above the area shown in this image. The high-energy radiation from these stars is sculpting the nebula's wall by slowly eroding it away.

2 - Southern Ring Nebula

Over thousands of years and before it became a white dwarf, the star periodically ejected mass the visible shells of material. As if on repeat, it contracted, heated up and then, unable to push out more material, pulsated. Stellar material was sent in all directions like a rotating sprinkler and provided the ingredients for this asymmetrical landscape. Today, the white dwarf is heating up the gas in the inner regions which appear blue at left and red at right. Both stars are lighting up the outer regions, shown in orange and blue, respectively.

3 - Webb's First Deep Field

First, focus on the galaxies responsible for the lensing: the bright white elliptical galaxy at the center of the image and smaller white galaxies throughout the image. Bound together by gravity in a galaxy cluster, they are bending the light from galaxies that appear in the vast distances behind them. The combined mass of the galaxies and dark matter act as a cosmic telescope, creating magnified, contorted, and sometimes mirrored images of individual galaxies.
